## Entity Overview

### Core Identity
Hanako-san (花子さん), meaning "Miss Hanako," is Japan's most famous school ghost and one of the most widespread urban legends among Japanese children. She is typically described as the spirit of a young girl who haunts school bathrooms, specifically appearing in the third stall from the entrance when summoned through a specific ritual. Despite variations in her backstory and appearance across different schools and regions, certain elements remain remarkably consistent: her manifestation in school restrooms, her red skirt, her association with the number three, and the ritual required to summon her.

Unlike many yokai or ghosts that exist in remote or historically significant locations, Hanako-san occupies an intensely familiar space—the school bathroom that every Japanese student uses daily. This proximity and accessibility make her legend particularly powerful and persistent among children and adolescents, serving as both a rite of passage in Japanese youth culture and a reflection of the anxieties associated with school life, peer pressure, and liminal spaces within educational institutions.

### Classification Details
- **Primary Type**: Ghost (Yūrei)
- **Specific Category**: School spirit/Urban legend ghost
- **Cultural Origin**: Japanese (Post-WWII urban legend)
- **Threat Level**: Low to moderate (mostly harmless but can be frightening)
- **Haunting Type**: Location-bound spirit (school bathrooms)
- **Activity Pattern**: Summoned through ritual
- **Moral Alignment**: Neutral to mildly malevolent (varies by account)

### Names and Variations
- **花子さん** (Hanako-san): Standard name, "Miss Hanako"
- **トイレの花子さん** (Toire no Hanako-san): "Hanako-san of the Toilet"
- **便所の花子さん** (Benjo no Hanako-san): "Hanako-san of the Bathroom" (older term)
- **三番目の花子さん** (Sanbanme no Hanako-san): "Third Stall Hanako-san"
- **Hanako of the Toilet**: English translation
- **花子ちゃん** (Hanako-chan): More informal, childlike version
- **赤い花子** (Akai Hanako): "Red Hanako" (emphasizing her red clothing)

## The Summoning Ritual

### Standard Procedure
Most common method to summon Hanako-san:

**Location Requirements**:
- School bathroom (usually girls' bathroom)
- Third floor preferred (but not always required)
- During or after school hours
- Empty bathroom (no other people present)
- Atmosphere of quiet and anticipation

**The Ritual Steps**:
1. Enter bathroom alone (very important)
2. Approach third stall from entrance
3. Knock three times on the stall door
4. Ask: "Are you there, Hanako-san?" (Hanako-san, irasshaimasu ka? / 花子さん、いらっしゃいますか?)
5. Wait for response
6. If voice answers "Yes" (Hai / はい), she is present
7. Opening the door reveals Hanako-san

**Variations**:
- Some versions require knocking on each stall sequentially
- Others specify certain times of day
- Number of knocks may vary (always three or multiples of three)
- Question phrasing slightly different by region
- Additional steps or words in some versions
- Order of actions important

### What Happens Next
Outcomes of successful summoning:

**Benign Encounters**:
- Hanako-san appears but does nothing harmful
- Brief sighting then disappearance
- Answers questions before vanishing
- Grants wish or provides information
- Peaceful if treated respectfully
- Proof of bravery for summoner

**Frightening Encounters**:
- Terrifying appearance causing panic
- Chases summoner from bathroom
- Grabs or touches summoner
- Screaming or aggressive behavior
- Psychological trauma from experience
- No physical harm but lasting fear

**Dangerous Encounters** (Rare):
- Drags summoner into toilet
- Pulls into supernatural realm
- Physical attack or injury
- Possesses summoner
- Summoner disappears or found injured
- Most extreme versions of legend

**Failed Summoning**:
- No response despite ritual
- Nothing happens at all
- Disappointment or relief
- Debate whether ritual done correctly
- Question of belief and expectation
- Most common outcome

Traditional advice about summoning:

## Conclusion

Hanako-san represents one of the most successful and enduring urban legends in Japanese culture, a story that has captivated generations of schoolchildren and evolved to remain relevant in contemporary society. Unlike ancient yokai with centuries of history, Hanako-san is a relatively modern creation, yet she has achieved a level of cultural penetration that rivals traditional folklore figures.

The legend's power lies in its perfect combination of elements: a familiar, everyday location (school bathrooms) made strange and frightening; a ritual that empowers children to test their courage while maintaining some control; and enough ambiguity to allow for regional variations and personal interpretations. The third stall in the girls' bathroom has become one of the most famous haunted locations in Japanese culture, despite existing in virtually every school in the country.

Hanako-san functions on multiple levels. For children, she provides a manageable scary story, a way to process fears about school, death, and the unknown in a controlled manner. For adolescents, she represents a rite of passage and a test of courage. For adults, she evokes nostalgia and serves as a connection to childhood. For society at large, she reflects anxieties about school safety, the vulnerability of children, and the lingering trauma of historical tragedies.

The legend has proven remarkably adaptable, surviving modernization of school facilities, changing cultural attitudes, increasing skepticism, and the rise of digital culture. Rather than fading away, Hanako-san has found new life in anime, manga, films, and internet horror stories, introducing her to international audiences while maintaining her essential character for Japanese children.

Whether Hanako-san is a ghost, a metaphor, a psychological phenomenon, or simply a story, her impact is undeniable. She has shaped the experience of Japanese childhood for decades, created a shared cultural touchstone across generations, and demonstrated that folklore continues to evolve and thrive even in our modern, rational, technological age. The girl in the red skirt in the third stall remains patiently waiting, ready to appear when summoned, a permanent fixture of Japanese school culture.
